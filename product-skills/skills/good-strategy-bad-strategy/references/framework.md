# Good Strategy / Bad Strategy: Detailed Framework Reference

Based on *Good Strategy Bad Strategy: The Difference and Why It Matters* by Richard Rumelt.

## Core Principle

A good strategy is a coherent set of analyses, concepts, policies, arguments, and actions that respond to a high-stakes challenge. It is NOT a set of goals, a vision statement, or a budget allocation. Strategy is the craft of figuring out the most important challenge and designing a way to address it.

## The Kernel of Good Strategy

Every good strategy has three elements. If any is missing, the strategy is incomplete.

### 1. Diagnosis

**Definition**: A clear, simplified description of the nature of the challenge. The diagnosis replaces overwhelmingly complex reality with a simpler story that identifies the most critical aspects of the situation.

**What a good diagnosis does**:
- Identifies the crux — the decisive point of the challenge
- Simplifies without oversimplifying — reduces complexity to its essential elements
- Names the obstacle — not just the goal, but what makes the goal hard to achieve
- Creates shared understanding — gives the organization a common frame for the situation
- Often reframes the problem — the obvious framing is frequently wrong

**Diagnostic Patterns**:
- **Analogy**: "This is like X" — maps the situation onto a known pattern
- **Root cause**: Traces symptoms back to fundamental drivers
- **Critical factor**: Identifies the 1-2 factors that matter more than everything else combined
- **Structural analysis**: Maps the forces, actors, and dynamics at play

**Common Diagnosis Failures**:
- Describing the goal instead of the challenge ("we need to grow 20%")
- Listing all problems without prioritizing
- Refusing to name uncomfortable truths
- Using industry jargon that obscures rather than clarifies
- Diagnosing yesterday's challenge instead of today's

### 2. Guiding Policy

**Definition**: An overall approach chosen to cope with or overcome the obstacles identified in the diagnosis. The guiding policy directs and constrains action without fully defining exactly what to do.

**What a good guiding policy does**:
- Channels action — provides a clear direction that rules out some actions and prioritizes others
- Creates advantage — focuses energy on the point of greatest leverage
- Exploits sources of strength — uses what you have, not what you wish you had
- Anticipates responses — accounts for how competitors, customers, or the market will react

**Guiding Policy Patterns**:
- **Focus**: Concentrate resources on a narrow front rather than spreading thin
- **Leverage**: Find the point where a small action creates a disproportionate effect
- **Proximate action**: Target achievable objectives that create momentum
- **Defense**: Protect critical positions while attacking elsewhere

**Common Guiding Policy Failures**:
- Too vague to guide action ("be customer-centric")
- A restatement of the goal ("our policy is to grow")
- A list of unrelated initiatives (not a single coherent direction)
- Internally contradictory (cut costs AND invest in everything)
- Ignores constraints (unlimited ambition, limited resources)

### 3. Coherent Actions

**Definition**: A set of coordinated, feasible actions designed to carry out the guiding policy. These are not individual to-dos — they are a system of moves that reinforce each other.

**What coherent actions look like**:
- **Coordinated**: Each action supports the others — they're designed as a set, not a list
- **Focused**: Resources are concentrated, not spread evenly across everything
- **Feasible**: Achievable with available resources, skills, and time
- **Proximate**: Close enough to be actionable — specific people, specific timelines, specific deliverables

**The Difference Between Actions and Goals**:
- Goal: "Improve retention"
- Action: "Redesign the Day 3-7 onboarding sequence to surface the aha moment before users churn, ship by March 15, measure 14-day retention lift"

**Coherence Tests**:
- Would removing one action weaken the others? (If not, it may not be part of the strategy.)
- Do the actions concentrate effort on the diagnosis? (If they spread across unrelated areas, they're not coherent.)
- Can each action be owned by a specific person or team? (If not, it's too abstract.)

## The Four Hallmarks of Bad Strategy

### 1. Fluff

Superficial restatement of the obvious using inflated language. Fluff uses buzzwords, jargon, and abstruse concepts to create the illusion of high-level thinking.

**How to detect**:
- Strip the adjectives and jargon. Is there a concrete idea underneath?
- Translate to plain language. Does it still say something meaningful?
- Ask "so what?" If the statement is equally true for any company in any situation, it's fluff.

**Examples**:
- "Our strategy is to be a customer-centric, innovation-led organization that leverages synergies across our portfolio."
- "We will drive transformational growth through operational excellence and strategic partnerships."

### 2. Failure to Face the Challenge

The strategy doesn't define the challenge. Without a clear diagnosis, the strategy can't be evaluated — there's no way to judge whether the actions address the problem.

**How to detect**:
- Can you state in one sentence what the core challenge is?
- Does the strategy acknowledge what's hard about the situation?
- Would this strategy change if the challenge were different? (If not, it's not addressing the challenge.)

### 3. Mistaking Goals for Strategy

Stating desired outcomes without explaining the approach. "We will be #1 in our market" is an aspiration, not a strategy. A strategy explains the logic of how you'll get there.

**How to detect**:
- Remove the numbers and timelines. Is there an approach left?
- Could you replace the goals with any other goals and the "strategy" would still work?
- Is there a "how" or just a "what"?

### 4. Bad Strategic Objectives

Objectives that fail to address the challenge, are internally contradictory, or are an exhaustive list of everything the organization wants to do.

**How to detect**:
- Count the priorities. More than 5 is a red flag. More than 10 is definitely not strategy.
- Check for contradictions. "Reduce costs AND invest aggressively in R&D AND expand to new markets AND improve margins" is four strategies pretending to be one.
- Check for connection to the diagnosis. Do the objectives address the specific challenge identified?

## Sources of Power in Strategy

### Leverage

Using a small action to produce a disproportionately large effect. Find the fulcrum — the point in the system where a nudge creates a cascade.

**Applied**: In product work, the fulcrum is often the bottleneck in the user journey. A small improvement at the activation step can cascade through retention, engagement, and revenue.

### Proximate Objectives

Objectives close enough to be feasible. A proximate objective is one where you can reasonably expect to achieve it — which creates momentum and positions you for the next move.

**The test**: Can you envision the specific actions that would accomplish this objective? If it requires "everything to go right," it's not proximate enough.

**Applied**: "Become the market leader" is not proximate. "Win the next 5 enterprise deals in financial services" is proximate — and each win teaches you something about the next.

### Chain-Link Systems

Systems where each component must be strong — a chain that breaks at its weakest link. The strategic implication: a chain-link system cannot be improved by strengthening only one link. It requires coordinated improvement across all links simultaneously.

**Why this matters competitively**: Chain-link systems are very difficult to replicate. A competitor can copy any single practice, but replicating a whole system of reinforcing practices requires changing everything at once — which is nearly impossible for an established organization.

**Applied**: Amazon's fulfillment system is chain-link: warehousing + logistics + software + labor management + vendor relationships. Copying any single piece doesn't help. You have to replicate the entire system.

### Focus

Coordinating policies and actions to concentrate effort on a decisive point. The opposite of focus is spreading resources evenly — which is the most common strategic error.

**Applied**: Rather than improving every feature a little, focus engineering effort on the single workflow that drives the most activation and make it exceptional. Rather than pursuing every market, dominate one and expand from a position of strength.

### Using Dynamics

Riding waves of change — technological shifts, regulatory changes, market transitions, demographic shifts — that amplify the effect of your actions. The key insight: during transitions, the established rules change, and the organizations that recognize the new dynamics first gain disproportionate advantage.

**Applied**: The shift to AI is a dynamic. Organizations that redesign their workflows around AI capabilities (not just bolt AI onto existing workflows) will gain structural advantage over those that treat AI as a feature addition.

## The Strategy Foundry Process

Rumelt's approach to developing strategy:

1. **List the challenges and opportunities** — Be comprehensive, then ruthless
2. **Determine the crux** — What's the single most important challenge? What makes it hard?
3. **Develop the diagnosis** — Simplify without oversimplifying. Name the obstacle.
4. **Create the guiding policy** — How will you address the crux? What direction? What constraints?
5. **Design coherent actions** — What coordinated moves carry out the policy?
6. **Test for coherence** — Do the pieces fit together? Do they concentrate effort? Are they feasible?
7. **Iterate** — Strategy development is not a one-pass process. Revisit the diagnosis as you learn.
