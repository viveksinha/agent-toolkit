# The 7 Powers: Detailed Framework Reference

## 1. Scale Economies

**Definition**: Per-unit costs decline as production volume increases.

**Key Attributes**:
- Cost advantage increases with scale
- Often present in businesses with high fixed costs and low variable costs
- Network effects can amplify scale economies

**Strategic Questions**:
- Does unit economics improve significantly with scale?
- What is the minimum efficient scale needed to achieve competitive costs?
- Can smaller competitors find niches where scale is less relevant?

**Examples**: Intel (fab investment), Google Search (per-query cost), Walmart (purchasing power), AWS (data center costs spread over customer base), Netflix (content costs spread over subscriber base).

## 2. Network Economies

**Definition**: The value of a product or service increases as more users adopt it.

**Key Attributes**:
- Value grows with user base (direct network effects)
- Value grows with complementary products/services (indirect network effects)
- Often creates winner-take-most dynamics

**Strategic Questions**:
- How strong are the network effects in this market?
- What is the critical mass needed for the network to be valuable?
- Are there opportunities for multi-homing that could reduce network power?

**Examples**: Facebook (social graph), Visa/Mastercard (two-sided merchant-consumer network), Windows (developer-user indirect effects), Uber (rider-driver density), eBay (buyer-seller marketplace).

## 3. Counter-Positioning

**Definition**: A newcomer adopts a superior business model that the incumbent cannot replicate due to anticipated damage to their existing business.

**Key Attributes**:
- Creates a damned-if-you-do, damned-if-you-don't dilemma for incumbents
- Often involves cannibalization of existing revenue streams
- Typically exploits technological or market shifts

**Strategic Questions**:
- Does the new approach fundamentally conflict with incumbents' business models?
- Would incumbents face significant revenue or margin decline by adopting this model?
- How long will the counter-positioning advantage last?

**Examples**: Netflix vs. Blockbuster (subscription vs. late fees), Kodak vs. digital photography, Charles Schwab vs. traditional brokerages (discount vs. advisory margins), Ryanair/Southwest vs. flag carriers (no-frills vs. premium hub-and-spoke), Robinhood vs. traditional brokerages (commission-free vs. trading fees), AWS vs. traditional IT vendors (pay-as-you-go vs. upfront enterprise sales).

## 4. Switching Costs

**Definition**: The costs (financial, procedural, relational, or emotional) that a customer incurs when changing from one product to another.

**Key Attributes**:
- Creates customer lock-in
- Can be technical, contractual, or habit-based
- Often increases over time as customers invest more in a solution

**Strategic Questions**:
- What are the monetary and non-monetary switching costs?
- How do switching costs evolve over the customer lifecycle?
- Are there ways to reduce customers' perception of switching costs?

**Examples**: Salesforce (workflow integration + retraining), Apple iOS ecosystem (apps, media, accessories, habits), Adobe Creative Cloud (workflow expertise + libraries), Amazon Prime (bundled benefits).

## 5. Branding

**Definition**: The durable attribution of higher value to an objectively identical offering that arises from historical information about the seller.

**Key Attributes**:
- Based on accumulated positive experiences and trust
- Most powerful when product quality is difficult to assess before purchase
- Requires consistent delivery of promised value

**Strategic Questions**:
- Is the brand associated with genuine quality differences?
- In which product attributes does the brand create perceived value?
- How difficult would it be for competitors to replicate the brand's associations?

**Examples**: Apple (innovation, premium design), Coca-Cola (nostalgia, global recognition), Nike (athletic excellence), Louis Vuitton (luxury, exclusivity), Tesla (cutting-edge technology, sustainability).

## 6. Cornered Resource

**Definition**: Preferential access to a coveted asset that is both unique and relevant to production.

**Key Attributes**:
- Can be physical (natural resources), intellectual (patents), or talent-based
- Must be difficult or impossible for competitors to obtain
- Must provide meaningful competitive advantage

**Strategic Questions**:
- How unique and valuable is the resource?
- How defensible is control over the resource?
- What is the durability of the resource advantage?

**Examples**: De Beers (diamond supply), pharmaceutical patents (Pfizer, Moderna), Google (proprietary search algorithms + data), Disney IP (Marvel, Star Wars, Pixar), TSMC (advanced semiconductor manufacturing know-how).

## 7. Process Power

**Definition**: Embedded company organization and activity sets which enable lower costs and/or superior products that rivals cannot replicate.

**Key Attributes**:
- Often developed through proprietary knowledge and continuous improvement
- Typically involves complex interdependencies between different processes
- Difficult to reverse-engineer from outside the organization

**Strategic Questions**:
- How difficult is it for competitors to replicate these processes?
- Are the processes properly documented and systematized?
- Does the process consistently deliver cost or quality advantages?

**Examples**: Toyota Production System (lean manufacturing, kaizen), Zara (fast fashion supply chain), Amazon Fulfillment (logistics automation), Southwest Airlines (standardized fleet, fast turnarounds), Intel (semiconductor fabrication innovation).

## Power Dynamics

### Power Progression
1. **Power Potential**: The conditions that make a Power possible
2. **Power Realization**: The development of the barrier
3. **Persistent Differential Returns**: The economic benefits that follow

### Timing Considerations
- **Origination**: When can a Power begin to form?
- **Takeoff**: When does the Power start delivering significant advantage?
- **Stability**: When does the Power reach maturity and maximum effectiveness?

## Power Combination Effects

Different Powers can reinforce each other:
- **Scale Economies + Network Effects**: Creates powerful growth loops
- **Switching Costs + Branding**: Reduces both rational and emotional incentives to switch
- **Counter-Positioning + Process Power**: Creates barriers both externally and internally
