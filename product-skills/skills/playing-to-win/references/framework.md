# Playing to Win: Detailed Framework Reference

Based on *Playing to Win: How Strategy Really Works* by A.G. Lafley and Roger Martin.

## Core Principle

Strategy is not a plan, a vision, or a set of goals. Strategy is an integrated set of choices that uniquely positions a company to create value and deliver it to customers in a way competitors can't easily replicate. The choices must be mutually reinforcing — each choice constrains and enables the others.

## The Strategy Cascade

### 1. Winning Aspiration

**Definition**: The purpose of the enterprise — the animating aspiration that sets the context for all strategic choices.

**Key Attributes**:
- Must be specific enough to guide choices, broad enough to inspire
- Defined in terms of customer value, not internal metrics
- "Winning" means being the clear best choice for your target customers — not just participating

**Diagnostic Questions**:
- What does winning look like for our customers?
- How would we know we've won? What would be measurably different?
- Is our aspiration ambitious enough to drive hard choices?
- Could everyone in the organization explain what winning means?

**Common Pitfall**: Aspirations that are so generic they provide no guidance. "Be the leading platform for X" tells you nothing. "Be the fastest way for mid-market B2B teams to go from idea to shipped experiment" tells you a lot.

### 2. Where to Play

**Definition**: The set of choices that narrow the competitive field — the specific arenas where you'll compete.

**Dimensions to Choose Along**:
- **Customer segments**: Which specific users or buyers? (demographics, firmographics, psychographics, jobs-to-be-done)
- **Geographies**: Which markets, regions, countries?
- **Product categories**: Which product lines, SKUs, service tiers?
- **Channels**: Direct, self-serve, partner, enterprise sales, marketplace?
- **Value chain stage**: R&D, manufacturing, distribution, retail, service?

**Key Attributes**:
- Where to Play is the most consequential choice — it determines who your competitors are
- Choosing where NOT to play is as important as choosing where to play
- The goal is to find arenas where your How to Win is most effective

**Diagnostic Questions**:
- Which customer segments value our differentiators the most?
- Where is the competition weakest relative to our strengths?
- Which arenas have structural profit pools worth winning?
- What are we explicitly choosing not to do?
- If we could only serve one segment, which would it be?

**Common Pitfall**: Choosing to play everywhere. "Our product is for everyone" means you have no strategy. Broad markets get narrowed by competitors who make sharper choices.

### 3. How to Win

**Definition**: The specific approach to winning within the chosen playing field — the unique value proposition.

**Two Generic Strategies** (Porter):
- **Cost leadership**: Deliver comparable value at lower cost than competitors
- **Differentiation**: Deliver unique value that justifies a premium

**Key Attributes**:
- Must be specific to the Where to Play choice — "how we win with mid-market B2B teams" not "how we win in general"
- Must create clear customer preference — customers should have an obvious reason to choose you
- Must be difficult for competitors to replicate

**Diagnostic Questions**:
- If a customer compared us side-by-side with the top alternative, why would they choose us?
- Can we state our advantage in one sentence without using buzzwords?
- Would a competitor agree this is our advantage? (If they'd dispute it, it's probably not real.)
- Is our advantage structural (built into the business model) or executional (just doing things better)?

**Common Pitfall**: Listing features instead of articulating the strategic logic. "We have AI-powered analytics" is a feature. "We turn raw product data into decisions faster than any alternative because our AI layer eliminates the analyst bottleneck" is a How to Win.

### 4. Core Capabilities

**Definition**: The reinforcing set of activities and competencies required to deliver the How to Win.

**Key Attributes**:
- Typically 3-5 capabilities, not 15
- Must be mutually reinforcing — each capability makes the others more powerful
- The activity system as a whole should be difficult to replicate, even if individual capabilities are not
- Distinguish between table-stakes capabilities (needed to compete) and distinctive capabilities (needed to win)

**Diagnostic Questions**:
- What 3-5 things must we be world-class at to deliver our How to Win?
- How do these capabilities reinforce each other?
- Which capabilities do we already have? Which must we build?
- Could a competitor replicate any single capability? Could they replicate the system?
- What's the gap between our current capabilities and what's needed?

**Common Pitfall**: Listing every function as a "core capability." If engineering, marketing, sales, support, AND design are all core, nothing is core. The point is to identify the specific capabilities where you must be disproportionately good.

### 5. Management Systems

**Definition**: The systems, structures, and measures that enable the capabilities and support the strategic choices.

**Categories**:
- **Measurement systems**: OKRs, dashboards, feedback loops, analytics
- **Operating processes**: Planning cadence, review rhythms, decision rights
- **Organizational structure**: Team design, reporting lines, cross-functional coordination
- **Talent systems**: Hiring profiles, development, incentive alignment
- **Culture and norms**: Decision-making principles, risk tolerance, speed vs. thoroughness

**Diagnostic Questions**:
- Do our measurement systems track progress on the capabilities that matter?
- Does our org structure enable or inhibit the key capabilities?
- Are we hiring for the capabilities we need to build?
- Do our incentives reward the behaviors our strategy requires?
- Do our planning and review processes reinforce strategic priorities?

**Common Pitfall**: Treating management systems as an afterthought. Strategy fails in execution more often than in conception. If the systems don't support the strategy, the strategy won't happen.

## Coherence Tests

The cascade is only as strong as its weakest link. Test for coherence:

1. **Vertical alignment**: Does each level flow logically from the one above? Capabilities should serve the How to Win, which should create advantage in the Where to Play, which should advance the Winning Aspiration.

2. **Mutual reinforcement**: Do the choices reinforce each other? The best strategies create a system where each choice makes the others stronger and harder to replicate.

3. **Competitor test**: Could a smart competitor read your strategy and know exactly how to beat you? If yes, your choices aren't distinctive enough. If they'd look at it and say "we can't do that because of our structure/model/positioning," you're on the right track.

4. **Inversion test**: What would the opposite strategy look like? If the opposite sounds equally plausible, you're making real choices. If the opposite sounds absurd ("we aspire to lose"), you're stating truisms, not making choices.

## The Strategy Logic Flow

For each major strategic choice, articulate:
- **What would have to be true** for this choice to be the right one?
- **What's the biggest risk** to this choice being wrong?
- **How would we know** if we need to revisit this choice?

This converts strategic choices into testable hypotheses — which turns strategy from a one-time exercise into a living system.
